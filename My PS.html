Maps Upon My Heart

What comes to mind when you hear the word "parasite"? Perhaps you picture a tiny organism thriving at the expense of its host, or maybe you think of the film that won the Palme d'Or at Cannes. For Hoa, it means both, and even more. It’s the word that once seemed to define her and her sister: two young girls crashing at an acquaintance's house, desperately searching for a place to belong.
From as early as she can remember, Hoa was aware that her family was different from others. They never had a home of their own. Growing up, she became accustomed to their constant search for a place to live, moving from one rented space to another, often sharing tight quarters. By the time she reached seventh grade, their small bedroom could no longer accommodate all of them, prompting her parents to arrange for Hoa and her sister to stay with their aunt. At first, it felt like happiness: having her own room and sleeping soundly without being cramped in a crowded bed. But no matter how hard they tried to make this live‑in arrangement work, it was all in vain. In the end, they had to concede that somehow, they were indeed "parasites".

The memory of that day remains as vivid to her as if it were yesterday. One frigid winter night, bundled in a cozy blanket and nose buried in her favorite novel, Hoa overheard her aunt on a phone call. She might have ignored it if she had not heard someone on the other end warning her aunt about the two girls, branding Hoa and her sister as “nothing more than two parasites who would only bring you trouble.” The word “parasites” struck her like a blow, leaving her momentarily speechless. Even at the age of twelve, she knew it was no compliment; it implied living off the resources of another person. And here that word was applied to them, two harmless little girls.

In a moment of disbelief, she fled the house, her mind numb and her hands trembling. She strolled aimlessly through the brightly lit streets as her thoughts swirled with questions: Why was this happening? Why did they deserve such disdain? Why were they even there? Tears started to stream down her face, blurring her vision. The night was biting, but she hardly felt it; the chill in the air was nothing compared to the coldness of that word, which had already dulled her senses. At that moment, something within her ignited-a fierce desire to change her life.

As she grew older, the sting of that word never fully left her, but it slowly changed shape. Instead of shame, it became a question that followed her everywhere: why did some children grow up feeling unwanted in someone else’s home? Bit by bit, she realized that it was not a matter of personal failure, but of circumstance, of families squeezed by low incomes and unstable housing, always one step away from having to move again. Rather than letting bitterness consume her, that once‑pathetic girl who had run crying into the winter night chose to fight for a different future: she studied hard, earned a degree from a good university, started her own small business, and, in time, created a home filled with warmth and security for three children who would never have to hear the word “parasite” in their own house. That is how Hoa became my mom.

A single story my mom shared at the dinner table did not end when she stopped; it stayed with me, resurfacing in quiet moments and quietly reshaping how I understood home and security. Over time, her memory stopped feeling like something that belonged only to her, but became a lens through which I saw families around me. Her unfinished wish, to see children grow up in a place that was truly theirs, gradually turned into a responsibility I claimed for myself, a commitment to help families like my mom’s find a home they would never have to apologize for.

Acknowledging that meaningful change should start from within, I devoted myself during high school not only to achieving academic excellence but also to various extracurricular activities that honed my skills and shaped my character. One of the highlights of this journey was Inspiration Day 2023, a program designed to welcome and motivate new students at my school. As a member of the Finance and External Relations Department, I was responsible for cultivating and maintaining strong relationships with our sponsors. This involved regular communication to ensure their continued support, addressing their needs and inquiries, and coordinating sponsorship agreements. Drawing from prior job experience, I managed these engagements effectively and successfully secured sponsorships from three major sponsors, which collectively contributed 40% of the funding needed for our annual event.

After a year, my commitment paid off when I was elected as the head of the department. Leading for the first time, I encountered numerous challenges, the biggest being a crucial moment when securing sponsors for our main events proved difficult. With only two weeks to go and no responses from the more than 100 businesses we had contacted, my team’s morale was at an all‑time low. As the deadline loomed, I felt the pressure mount. Without sponsorships, our event would suffer, possibly forcing us to increase the participation fee, which could significantly reduce attendance.

Determined to overcome this setback, I rallied my team and reworked our strategy. We shifted from mass email outreach to focusing on a targeted list of the five most promising prospects, reaching out directly to their managers with personalized proposals. Eventually, we received replies requesting more details, and soon, all five businesses engaged in negotiations with us. In the end, we secured sponsorships totaling 50 million VND, allowing us to host a successful event for 600 participants at an affordable entry fee.

Through these experiences, I learned the value of resilience and determination. Even in the face of rejection, hope persists. The dream of stability—a home, a future—is never out of reach for those willing to persevere.

Now, my ambition extends far beyond myself. I am passionate about exploring how thoughtful, sustainable real estate solutions can transform lives and communities. My experiences in organizing, negotiating, and leading have shaped my ability to contribute meaningfully to collaborative projects, tackle complex challenges, and develop new ideas. By embracing every opportunity to learn and participate, I hope to turn both my mom’s history and my own into purposeful action, creating homes where none existed before, and helping others find the sense of belonging she once longed for and I grew up determined to protect.
